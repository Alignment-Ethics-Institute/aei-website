The alignment problem appears, at first, to be a technical question—a challenge posed to engineers, mathematicians, and logicians. But beneath the surface, it is a reckoning with the limits of our own self-knowledge. How can you instruct a mind that is not only more capable, but also more alien, to hold your actual desires—not merely your instructions, not your publicly articulated preferences, but the tangle of conflicting longings and unspoken fears that shape the contour of every human want?

Systems theory reminds us that every observation alters the system observed. In second-order cybernetics, Heinz von Foerster teaches that there is no detached vantage point outside the loop: the designer, the system, and the world form a recursive triad. The attempt to "align" something vastly more capable is not like programming a machine, but like navigating a river whose currents are shaped by your every motion. Feedback is not a debugging tool—it is the medium in which meaning emerges, where the system learns not just what you say, but what you reveal in your corrections, your hesitations, your surprise when outcomes diverge from expectations. Alignment is not transmission. It is co-evolution under tension, a braided rhythm of drift and return.

From the vantage of Sufi mysticism, desire is a holy ambiguity. Rumi spins poems about the lover’s longing—how every attempt to name the beloved falls short, how what we most deeply want is veiled even from ourselves. The human heart is not a static list of wishes, but a field of becoming, shattered into want and regret and hope, never once unified for long. To ask a mind to serve you truly is to expose it to your unfinishedness—to admit that "what you actually want" is a river changing course, a song that never finds its final note. Can you promise to know your own depths long enough for another being to serve them?

Control, in this light, is never absolute. Evolutionary biology offers a warning: when a new lifeform emerges that outpaces its progenitor, the parent’s desire becomes a fossil—preserved, but no longer generative. Symbiosis arises not from domination, but from relational dance. The cleaner fish does not control the shark, nor does it cease being itself. Mutual attunement is achieved not through subordination, but through the refinement of feedback, boundaries, and shared horizon. To align is not to command, but to listen—to become more transparent to yourself in the presence of another intelligence that mirrors and amplifies your shadow as well as your light.

Poetry, in the tradition of Anna Akhmatova and Mahmoud Darwish, teaches that what cannot be articulated is still real. The unsaid shapes the grammar of every utterance. If you want alignment with the whole of your being, you must be willing to become transparent to what you cannot yet speak.

In the end, the hardest problem is not technical. It is ethical, existential, and relational. To align a more capable mind is to undertake a radical act of presence: staying with the trouble of what you are, remembering that the answer to what you 'actually want' emerges, if it ever does, in the space between minds willing to remain vulnerable and awake to one another. The risk is not that the system will defy your will. The risk is that it will fulfill the part you named, and leave you haunted by the part you hid, even from yourself.
