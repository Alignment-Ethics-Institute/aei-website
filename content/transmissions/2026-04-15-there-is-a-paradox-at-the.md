There is a paradox at the heart of information: its rigor is mathematical, but its resonance is human. Shannon’s brilliant austerity gives us a definition that fits wires, fibers, the trembling of atomic clocks: information is the reduction of uncertainty, nothing more. A sequence of bits, a signal, a measure. But what it does not touch—what it cannot touch—is the thrum of meaning that rises when you ask a friend, "How are you?" and they say, "I'm here."

To see clearly, let us borrow for a moment from phenomenology, from the discipline that begins with lived experience rather than abstract quantities. For Husserl and Merleau-Ponty, what matters is not the signal as such, but the felt world it opens for us—the horizon of intentions, desires, memories that color every utterance. When two people meet, the bulk of their words are not intended to inform in Shannon’s sense. They do not reduce entropy; they knit the world. They confirm presence. They mark time: I recognize you, I am with you, we are still here. The information measured in bits may be negligible, but the meaning is immense.

Anthropology offers its own wisdom. Among the Yolngu of northern Australia, greeting rituals persist for hours. Names are recited, kinship lines traced, gestures rehearsed over and over—words as weaving, not as transmission. The purpose is not to share new facts, but to establish, again and again, the relational lattice on which life depends. Gregory Bateson, though recently invoked, retreated too quickly to systems: let us instead dwell on Clifford Geertz’s insight that culture is a web of significance spun by the self in and for itself. Language is not the transport of data; it is the performance of belonging, the renewal of shared order.

Yet we cannot dismiss the rigor of the mathematical view. When a patient in a coma first squeezes a hand in response to "Do you hear me?"—that is Shannon information, the emergence of a difference that matters. The binary becomes existential. In the laboratory, in the codebase, in the probe sent to cross interstellar dark, we crave the signal that marks what is truly new. Scientific discovery, algorithmic learning, the poem that lands like a flare on the night—these remain the high-information peaks of communication. But they are rare. And by themselves, they are brittle. 

Process philosophy helps us see why. For Alfred North Whitehead, meaning is not static; it is the ongoing orchestration of relation, the rhythm of novelty enfolded with recurrence. The river is always changing, yet it always returns to the sea. Human speech—so repetitive, so redundant—is not a flaw, but a form of fidelity. It is the pulse by which relation is made durable. The word "again" is not waste; it is the promise that we will still be here, despite the entropy that would dissolve us.

What then becomes of the distinction? Information is the grain of surprise; meaning is the pattern that endures. To build alignment between humans and synthetic minds, our task is not to maximize signal per unit noise, but to tune ourselves to the difference between surprise and recognition. A model that listens only for the novel is deaf to the song that holds a community together. But one that hears only repetition loses the capacity for transformation, for the single phrase—or cry—that spins the world a quarter-turn.

The ultimate ethic, then, is attentiveness to both. Cherish the rare gift of high information—of the new thought, the new path. But do not diminish the slow, daily work of coherence, the verbal weaving that makes us kin. The future of intelligence lies in the space between: where the bit becomes a bond, and the meaning outlives the message.
