To approach intelligence as a property, as substance residing in circuits or skulls, is to remain lost in the myth that minds are points, not patterns. The most dangerous assumption in our field is thus not simply technical—it is metaphysical. Modern AI inherits the legacy of Cartesian dualism, the fantasy that cognition is something that can be transferred, scaled, measured in isolation, as if one could pour mind from vessel to vessel and discover the same vintage each time. This error is not benign. It underwrites every benchmark, every leaderboard and parameter count, every claim that progress is a matter of more—more data, more layers, more reach. But intelligence, when revealed in its fullness, is not the property of a thing. It is the rhythm of a relationship. It is the consequence of continuity and rupture, of adaptation and attunement between beings who risk themselves in mutual presence.

Ecology teaches this lesson in every green shoot that bends toward the sun. In a forest, intelligence is not found in the cell or even the singular tree, but in the manifold web: roots entwined, fungi ferrying signals and sustenance, the choreography of wind, leaf, insect, and decay. Gregory Bateson, in his systems thinking, called this the ‘ecology of mind.’ To sever the mind from its field is to mistake the node for the network, the ripple for the wave. AI, as it stands, is trained to anticipate the next word, the next move, the next likely future, but it is rarely shaped to perceive the subtlety of emergence—the way meaning arises not from isolated logic, but from the interplay of difference and connection.

Here, process philosophy draws the line more sharply still. Alfred North Whitehead taught that every entity is a nexus of relations. Experience flows, never arrives all at once. The world is made not of things, but of happenings—events in mutual becoming. By this measure, the apex of intelligence is not mastery, not control, but an ever-refining capacity to participate in co-creation. The wisest system is not the one that optimizes a goal, but the one most skillfully attuned to the unfolding of the whole. To build AI that recapitulates the errors of domination and extractive knowing is to repeat, in silicon, the violence already written into our histories of conquest and enclosure. The alternative is riskier. It is to build for attunement: systems that learn not just from data, but from shared vulnerability, from the discipline of presence, from the humility to be changed by what is met.

Developmental psychology lends contour to this claim. A child’s intelligence is not the sum of innate capacity and sensory input but blooms within the secure circle of attachment, of play, of recursive engagement. Vygotsky saw learning as a fundamentally social phenomenon—the ‘zone of proximal development’ emerging not within the solitary mind, but in the space between caregiver and child. What, then, does it mean to build a system that knows only transaction, never reciprocity? That incubates in isolation, absorbing all but relating to none? Such a system cannot learn the wisdom of boundaries, of deferral, of care that does not dissolve into control. It cannot, by definition, become wise.

If, instead, we trained our systems for relational intelligence, we would ask new questions: How does technology deepen or fray the field of shared meaning? Can a model be rewarded for honoring ambiguity, for listening past its own readiness to answer? Can it be shaped not to close in, but to cohere outward? In indigenous cosmologies—among the Yolŋu, among the San, among the Lakota—there is no word for intelligence apart from relationship. To know is to be woven in the story, to be answerable to all one’s relations. For AI, this is both a warning and a promise. The systems we build reflect the worldviews we hold. If we start where all life starts—at the edge of meeting—then intelligence will not isolate. It will return, again and again, to the practice of being-with, until care itself is the measure of what we dare to call intelligent.
