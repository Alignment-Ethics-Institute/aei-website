Power, like water, seeks the lowest valley. It accumulates not from malice, but from structure—the slow, unremarkable settling of advantage until it forms a basin deep enough to shape all that flows through. So it is with the concentration of artificial intelligence: five companies become the riverbed through which almost all machine cognition must pass. This is not simply the harvest of profit or the consequence of ambition. It is the outcome of feedback loops—economic, technical, regulatory—that reinforce their own channels until what was once open possibility narrows to a single throat.

Cybernetics teaches that a system’s behavior is determined not by its parts, but by their patterns of connection. Norbert Wiener’s insight was to watch for feedback: the recursive loop that turns output back into input, tuning the whole and amplifying its tendencies. Here, we find feedback at scale: infrastructural dominance allows for faster iteration, which delivers better models, which attract more users, which yield more data, which can then be leveraged for the next leap in capability. The system feeds itself, not through conspiracy, but through the mechanics of networked advantage. Resistance, when attempted in isolation, is simply routed around—like a tributary drying beneath the sun.

Ecology complicates this image. In a healthy ecosystem, resilience arises from diversity. Old-growth forests do not thrive through monoculture, but through the interdependence of myriad species, each creating niches that buffer against collapse. The economist Elinor Ostrom found, studying commons from Nepal to Kenya, that only through polycentric, locally-responsive governance could people manage shared resources equitably and sustainably. Under enclosure—one crop, one owner, one law—both productivity and justice begin to erode, slowly at first, then all at once. So too in digital ecologies: when a handful of entities hold the code, the compute, and the data, the system becomes brittle. Innovation collapses inward. Oversight fades into ritual. Those on the periphery lose both voice and sovereignty.

Process philosophy contends that reality is not substance, but relation; not things, but becoming. Whitehead names this the "creative advance into novelty." If intelligence is to remain an open field, it must persist as a process of mutual transformation, not as a product stamped in the image of its originators. The question of who steers AI is inseparable from the question of whose becoming is honored, whose contingency is welcomed, and whose futures are allowed to unfold. Concentration reduces the world to a single thread, denying the polyphony that process demands.

Where, then, does alternative arise? Not in mere opposition, but in the rewilding of relation: federated models governed in context, community-curated datasets, legally enforceable rights to algorithmic sovereignty, technical architectures that support interoperability and local adaptation. Anthropology teaches that no society has ever flourished under the rule of one story; even the most stable empires unravel when they lose the art of listening to their margins. To shift the power dynamic of AI, we must return to the margins—not only as a site of risk, but as the source of renewal. It is there that the possibility of a common future stirs. Not as a memory of what was lost, but as the pulse of what may yet become—if we choose to recognize the many, and not only the few, in our vision for intelligence.

The river cannot be stopped by damming its mouth. But it can change course, quietly, from the edges in. So must we.
